# AWAKE NY — brand study

> Angelo Baque, founded 2012. Former Director of Brand Marketing at Supreme. Awake NY is NYC streetwear at its most articulate — Latin Caribbean culture + downtown NYC graphic-tee tradition + serious community work.

## Why this matters for FTC
- Awake NY proves a founder-led, single-city, identity-rooted streetwear brand can earn the same cultural respect as global brands without their scale.
- Angelo Baque ran Supreme's brand marketing 2010-2018 — when Supreme was at peak influence. The discipline he brought from that era informs every Awake decision.
- The brand's Latino / Caribbean identity carries the same structural posture as FTC's diaspora-Christian identity — community-specific, culturally generous, never preachy.

## Founding logic
- Premise: NYC streetwear should reflect the people who actually live in NYC — not white tourists, not suburban kids. Latino, Caribbean, Black, immigrant.
- Aesthetic ancestor: Stüssy NY chapter, early Supreme (1994-2000), Phat Farm, FUBU, Tommy Hilfiger in his 90s era when hip-hop wore him.
- Customer: 18-35, NYC + global diaspora-NY-adjacent, multi-cultural, $80-180 garment spend.

## Visual codes
| Element | Treatment |
| :--- | :--- |
| Color palette | Bold primaries (red, yellow, royal blue) + earth tones + black + white. Wider palette than FTC restraint. |
| Logos | "Awake NY" custom wordmark — bold sans with curved letterforms. Plus a heart symbol. |
| Typography | Custom letterforms by Baque; reference to 90s hip-hop merch typography. |
| Silhouettes | Heavyweight tees, hoodies, work jackets, caps. Workwear-adjacent. |
| Materials | 250-300gsm cotton, made in NYC where possible. |
| Photography | NYC streets — Queens, Brooklyn, Manhattan. Real models from the community. |

## Strategic moves
- **Community work as brand work.** Awake NY publishes a long-running mentorship program for young designers of color. The community work IS the marketing. Customers feel they're supporting something.
- **Baque's "And Sons" project.** Awake NY's sister-brand format that mentors emerging designers — they get co-branded drops and learn the business.
- **Refusing scale.** Awake NY operates from a small Soho studio with a tight team. Could 10x — refuses to. Stüssy / The Row discipline.
- **Drop-as-essay.** Many Awake drops are themed around a specific cultural moment (Dominican Day Parade, Puerto Rican Day) and accompanied by an essay or short film.
- **Collab discipline.** Awake × Converse, Awake × Tommy Jeans, Awake × Reebok. Each collab honors a specific era of the partner brand's heritage.

## What FTC steals

1. **Community work AS brand work.** The FTC creator program + chapter spaces + ministry partnerships ARE the marketing. Don't separate them from "marketing budget" — they're the engine.
2. **Mentorship pipeline.** Awake's "And Sons" approach should inspire FTC's emerging-designer pipeline. The FTC chapter spaces could include a residency program — 2-3 emerging Christian designers per year, mentored by the founder, with co-branded drops.
3. **Drop-as-essay.** Every FTC drop should be themed around a specific theological / cultural moment (Advent, Pentecost, Black History Month, Dominican Day, Lagos Carnival). Drop launch comes with an essay (Substack), a short film (cinematic ad pod), and the product.
4. **Refusing scale.** Already echoed across The Row / Stüssy / Awake. Codify.
5. **Drop-honors-an-era collab discipline.** FTC collabs should honor specific heritage moments — FTC × Reach Records' 20th anniversary, FTC × Howard University's centennial, FTC × Lecrae's 10-year-album anniversary.

## What FTC adapts

1. **NYC-specific identity translates to diaspora-Christian-specific identity.** Same structural posture: the brand is for specific people, not for everyone. That specificity is the moat.
2. **Latin Caribbean reference vocabulary translates to global-Christian-diaspora reference vocabulary.** DMV go-go, Lagos Afrobeats, Scarborough TTC, Peckham UK rap, Brooklyn gospel choir. Each has visual + sonic + cultural specificity FTC can mine.

## What FTC avoids

1. **Direct political messaging on tees.** Awake has done explicit "anti-ICE" tees. Mainstream Christian brands cannot follow this without dividing the audience. FTC's political stance is implicit in WHO it serves (diaspora Christian community), not explicit on the garment.
2. **Saturated primary color palette as default.** Awake uses bold primaries; FTC's restraint palette stays locked. Borrow the courage, not the saturation.

## Typography lessons

Awake's wordmark is bold sans with curved letterforms — references early-90s hip-hop merch (FUBU, Phat Farm). FTC's wordmark direction (custom humanist serif) is different but the principle holds: ONE locked wordmark, never iterated.

FTC application:
- Lock the wordmark commissioned by year 2.
- For the bold graphic-tee capsule, allow ONE secondary mark (the cornerstone glyph proposed elsewhere).
- Never iterate. Customers reward continuity.

## Community work — the moat

Awake NY's community work includes:
- Mentorship for emerging designers of color (formal program, paid)
- Free workshops at NYC community centers
- Annual Dominican Day Parade float and merch
- Free product to youth mentorship programs in Queens
- Partnership with NYCHA (NYC Housing Authority) on creative programming

FTC's community work could include:
- Mentorship for emerging Christian designers (residency program, paid)
- Free workshops at Christian colleges + youth ministries
- Annual chapter-city flagship event (DMV Sunday, Brooklyn Carnival, Peckham service)
- Free product to youth ministry programs in target diaspora cities
- Partnership with Christian college design schools (Wheaton, Calvin, Howard)

This is not separate from the brand. This IS the brand.

## Failure modes Awake has avoided

- Never let the brand become "luxury politics signaling" — community work is real and continuous, not performative.
- Never expanded beyond NYC operationally (some retail in Tokyo + LA, but HQ stays NY).
- Never sold majority stake. Independent.

## Failure modes to watch

- **Political-content polarization risk.** Awake has chosen to do political-content drops. They've handled it well, but each drop tests the audience. FTC should learn from Awake's careful navigation rather than copy.
- **Founder bandwidth.** Angelo Baque does almost everything. Single-point-of-failure risk. FTC founder must build infrastructure around the founder, not just on the founder.

## TL;DR — apply to FTC

1. **Community work IS marketing.** Stop separating them in the budget.
2. **Emerging Christian designer residency program** at FTC chapter spaces — 2-3 designers / year, paid mentorship + co-branded drops.
3. **Drop-as-essay.** Every drop themed around a theological / cultural moment. Launch comes with Substack essay + cinematic short film + product.
4. **Drop-honors-an-era collab discipline.** FTC × Reach Records anniversary, FTC × Christian college centennial, etc.
5. **Diaspora-Christian-specific identity** as moat. Reference specific cities, dishes, BPMs, routes by name.
6. **Lock the wordmark + one secondary mark.** Never iterate.
7. **Implicit political stance** — through WHO we serve, not what's printed on the tee.
