# BRAIN DEAD — brand study

> Kyle Ng + Ed Davis, founded 2014 in Los Angeles. Collage zine aesthetic as luxury streetwear. Heavy halftone print, vintage horror movie type, sci-fi B-movie references. Most cited "thinking person's streetwear" of the 2010s-2020s.

## Why this matters for FTC
- Brain Dead proves that intellectual references (Stan Brakhage films, William Gibson novels, Antonioni's Italian cinema) can become graphic-tee content WITHOUT becoming pretentious.
- The collage-zine aesthetic is a direct visual cousin to medieval Christian manuscript marginalia. FTC's Sermon-tier could lean on this lineage explicitly.
- Brain Dead's collaboration list (A24, Converse, The North Face, Adidas, Stüssy, MoMA) demonstrates how a graphic-driven brand earns serious cultural partners.

## Founding logic
- Premise: graphic tees can be intellectual without being snobbish. Real references, layered visuals.
- Aesthetic ancestor: 1970s-1980s underground zine culture, riso-print revival, vintage horror movie poster grammar (Hammer Films, Italian giallo cinema), early Adobe magazine layouts.
- Customer: 22-40, college-educated, reads books, watches Criterion films, $80-200 graphic-tee spend.

## Visual codes
| Element | Treatment |
| :--- | :--- |
| Color palette | Muted saturation + accent colors. Forest green, rust, ochre, ash. Limited primaries. |
| Logos | "Brain Dead" wordmark in custom sans + a hand-drawn smiley-with-X-eyes "BD" mascot. |
| Typography | Halftone-printed serifs, condensed sans, vintage horror movie type, occasional Trajan. |
| Silhouettes | Standard heavyweight tees, hoodies, work pants. Garment plain; graphic is the work. |
| Materials | 250-300gsm cotton, riso-print or screen-print, occasional textured nylon. |
| Photography | Heavy collage; cut-and-paste; photo-overlay. The marketing aesthetic IS the product aesthetic. |

## Strategic moves
- **Layered visual references per tee.** A single Brain Dead tee might combine: a movie still from a 1970s giallo + hand-drawn type + a Latin phrase + a halftone background pattern. Density as design strategy.
- **Magazine + product fusion.** Brain Dead's website reads like an underground magazine. Editorials, interviews, film essays, then product. Lowers the commerce-feel.
- **Curatorial collabs.** Brain Dead × A24 means the brand consciously partners with cultural institutions that share the intellectual posture. Each collab elevates rather than dilutes.
- **In-store events.** The LA flagship hosts film screenings, panel discussions, zine launches. The store is community space.
- **Risograph + screen print.** Print process is part of the design — riso's color limitations + registration imperfections become brand signal.

## What FTC steals

1. **Layered references as graphic-tee strategy.** FTC tees can stack: a manuscript marginalia + a hymn fragment + a tonal pattern + a Latin phrase. Density done right reads intellectual, not cluttered.
2. **Magazine + product fusion** on the website. FTC's Shopify should incorporate editorial content directly into the catalog flow.
3. **Curatorial collabs.** FTC × A24 type partnerships in the Christian-cultural space — FTC × Reach Records, FTC × a Christian college press, FTC × a Christian magazine (Plough? Comment?). Lift the brand into cultural-institution territory.
4. **In-store / chapter events.** FTC chapter spaces should host: theology + design panel discussions, gospel-rap album listening parties, Christian college design school exhibitions.
5. **Riso-print aesthetic.** The print process becomes a visual signature. FTC could commission a riso-print series for one annual capsule — the imperfections become the mark.
6. **Vintage horror typography reference** can translate to **vintage hymn-book typography reference**. Trajan, blackletter (carefully — see Geedup cautionary), illuminated manuscript marginalia. Christian historical type traditions are deep.

## What FTC adapts (not direct steals)

1. **Latin phrases used decoratively.** Brain Dead uses Latin / French / Italian phrases as design elements. FTC could use Greek (New Testament Greek), Hebrew, Latin (Vulgate Bible Latin) the same way — without translating them on the tee. Customer Googles, learns, deepens relationship to the brand.
2. **B-movie reference culture** translates to **prophet / apocrypha / hagiography reference culture**. Augustine confessions, Julian of Norwich, Brother Lawrence's *Practice of the Presence of God*. Deep wells of imagery and text.

## What FTC avoids

1. **Pure horror / occult aesthetics.** Brain Dead leans on horror movie grammar that overlaps with FTC's Section 1 forbidden list (skulls, occult). Take the LAYOUT discipline, skip the iconography.
2. **Ironic intellectual reference.** Brain Dead's references are sincere but the visual treatment sometimes reads ironic. FTC must stay sincere.
3. **Sci-fi dystopian content.** Brain Dead's William Gibson references work for them; not FTC's lane.

## Typography lessons

Brain Dead uses:
- Halftone-printed condensed serif headlines (newspaper / magazine register)
- Trajan for some monumental type pieces
- Custom-drawn vintage-horror typefaces for select drops
- Body type in a clean grotesque

FTC application:
- Use halftone print as a texture device on select graphic tees
- Use Trajan for monumental theology references ("EVERY GOOD GIFT") at small scale
- Commission custom-drawn manuscript-marginalia-inspired typefaces for limited capsules
- Continue Inter / Founders Grotesk for body type

## Print process as identity (the riso-print lesson)

Brain Dead's risograph aesthetic isn't just visual — it's economic. Riso prints have:
- 2-4 colors max per design (the riso machine limits)
- Color misregistration (intentional, charming)
- Texture from the riso paper
- Sub-$1 per print cost (vs $3-5 for screen print)

FTC could adopt riso for: select editorial capsules + chapter event posters + Substack subscriber gifts. The economics open up small-batch / community-gift programs that screen print can't justify.

## Failure modes Brain Dead has avoided

- Never over-explained the references. Customers Google. The hunt is part of the relationship.
- Never expanded into footwear (the Converse collab is rare exception). Lane discipline.
- Never let the LA flagship become tourist attraction. Stayed neighborhood-scale.

## Failure modes to watch

- **Reference fatigue.** Customers can tire of figuring out what film a graphic references. Some pieces should stand alone visually.
- **Print-process snobbery.** Riso fans can become insufferable about it. Use the technique without the lecture.

## TL;DR — apply to FTC

1. **Layered references as graphic-tee strategy.** Stack manuscript marginalia + hymn fragment + tonal pattern + Greek/Hebrew/Latin phrase.
2. **Magazine + product fusion** on FTC's Shopify and Substack.
3. **Curatorial collabs in Christian-cultural space** — Reach Records, Plough magazine, Christian college press, Christian magazine.
4. **In-store / chapter event programming** — theology + design panels, listening parties, exhibitions.
5. **Riso-print aesthetic for one annual capsule** — small batch, imperfect registration, chapter / Substack subscriber gifts.
6. **Greek / Hebrew / Latin phrases as design elements** — sincere not ironic. No translations on the tee.
7. **Manuscript-marginalia and illuminated-letter typography references** — Christian historical type traditions instead of horror typography.
